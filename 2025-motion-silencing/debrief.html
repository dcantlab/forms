The Role of Stimulus Features in the Motion Silencing Illusion — Background and Purpose of the Study

Background. Imagine small visual elements arranged randomly around a fixation point. Changes in their attributes, such as color, size, and shape, are easily noticeable. However, when these changes coincide with the movement of the visual elements, they become much less noticeable. Suchow and Alvarez (2011) coined the term "motion silencing" to indicate the inability to describe these changes. Wu et al. (2024) suggested that this illusion occurs not because the motion itself hinders the color detection, but the rotational motion makes it difficult to bind visual feature changes to specific visual elements when they are in motion. However, this suggestion does not account for previous findings that translational motion can also cause the motion silencing illusion (Pierce, 2013), even though the effect is less pronounced. That is, rotational motion isnot the only cause of motion silencing.

Purpose. We seek to understand changes in which visual features can be detected during the motion silencing illusion and which visual features can be effectively silenced by the motion silencing illusion.

References.

  • Wu, Q., & Flombaum, J. I. (2024). The Motion-Silencing Illusion Depends on Object-Centered Representation. Psychological Science, 35(5), 504–516.
